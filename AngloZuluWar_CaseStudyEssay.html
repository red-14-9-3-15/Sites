Once a Nation of Kingdoms, now a Nation of Peace

(Intro)

The Anglo-Zulu War, which took place in 1879, was fought between the Zulu kingdom and the British Empire. The British Empire defeated the Zulu kingdoms, but not without a serious loss. This War entails fighting between an incredibly advanced Empire and a Kingdom of men whose best weaponry was old muskets that had to be purchased shortly before the war to stand a chance against the British Empire. The most prominent battles were the Battle of Isandlwana and the defence of Rorke’s Drift. When the British Empire claimed the land that had once belonged to the Zulu kingdom during the Berlin conference, the Zulu warriors were understandably quite angry. When the British Soldiers entered their unrightfully claimed land, they came across many tribes of men who were vastly inferior in terms of weaponry compared to the British. They were typically using spears and shields compared to the British guns. These tribes fell rather quickly, which allowed the British to gain land, resources, and power in Africa. When the land was taken, they turned their sights on one of the largest kingdoms in all of Africa, the Zulu kingdom. The Zulu influence on the surrounding areas made it a prime target for the British. The Zulus wanted peace; their king, Cetshwayo kaMpande, repeatedly told the British they had no intention of fighting the British unless they were provoked. The British leader wanted glory and thus sent Lord Chelmsford to defeat the Zulu Empire.

(Historical evidence 1)

Before the British had even entered Africa, a conference was held that would change the course of History forever. The Berlin Conference was the point when the Leading European powers came together to colonize and split up the resource-rich Africa between themselves. The countries that signed this act which gave the rights of Africa tot he major European powers to do whatever they wanted were, “Signed by the representatives of the United Kingdom, France, Germany, Austria, Belgium, Denmark, Spain, the United States of America, Italy, the Netherlands, Portugal, Russia, Sweden-Norway, and Turkey (Ottoman Empire).” (Page 1, General Act of the Berlin Conference on West Africa, 26 February 1885) Only a handful of countries got territory; the others, like the newly formed United States of America, were there just to watch and make sure the African country of Liberia would go untouched, as this country was created by escaped/freed slaves who were once from America. These Countries did not participate in the creation of borders, nor did they receive any land in Africa. The Berlin conference marked the beginning of the colonization of Africa. “No African nations were invited or represented.” (Colonising Africa: What happened at the Berlin Conference of …)

(Historical evedince 2)

An ultimatum was sent out to the Zulus when the British finally decided to take on the kingdom. The ultimatum was answered by, first, a suggestion of peace; however, realizing the British would have no peace, they finally answered, and they protected their land. The battle of Isandlwana began when a large British force made camp, the Zulus took advantage of their relaxed defenses and made a surprise attack, the British were not prepared fo the fight and scrambled to get their weapons, a first hand account of one of the lieutenants “Soon after 2 A.M. on the 22nd January I received instructions from the Lieutenant-General to send a written order to Lieutenant-Colonel Durnford, R.E., commanding No. 2 Column, to the following effect (I copied it in my note-book which was afterwards lost): " Move up to Sandhlwana Camp at once with all your mounted men and Rocket Battery—take command of it. I am accompanying Colonel Glyn, who is moving off at once to attack Matyana and a Zulu force” (https://www.northeastmedals.co.uk/britishguide/zulu/despatch6_isandhlwana_isandlwana.htm)  however, they could not kill enough Zulus, the British had been defeated. The commanders of the battle were Lieutenant Colonel Pulleine of the 24th Foot, and Lieutenant Colonel Durnford commanded the British force at the battle. The Zulu Army was commanded by Chiefs Ntshingwayo kaMahole and Mavumengwana kaMdlela Ntuli.” (https://www.britishbattles.com/zulu-war/battle-of-isandlwana/) The British were heavily outnumbered, but they had the advanced weaponry. This battle would be the biggest Zulu victory of the war. The British were forced to retreat as the Zulus followed suit, engaging the British in any way they could as the Empire fled back to friendly lines. This defeat would make its way back to the person in command of the whole British operation in Africa, and he was not pleased. The British had to retreat out of Zululand until a much larger British force could regain all the lost territory.

(Historical evidence 3)

Moving to a closer time, South Africa remains one of the only countries that has been able to have Europeans and Africans, as well as all other ethnicities, live together. It was also the only country that was able to dismantle its nuclear program. “On 26 February 1990, President Frederik Willem de Klerk ordered the destruction of the six completed nuclear weapons and the seventh partially completed device. President de Klerk announced to South Africa’s Parliament on 24 March 1993 the existence and abandonment of the former nuclear weapons program. IAEA inspections between April and August 1993 confirmed the complete dismantlement of the nuclear weapons program. As of 2015, South Africa still possessed approximately 185 lbs. of HEU in a central facility under IAEA safeguards. Recently, South Africa has emerged as a leader of the movement to implement and expand nuclear energy throughout Africa. South Africa currently has one nuclear power reactor, and it is the only one on the continent.” (https://www.nti.org/analysis/articles/south-africa-nuclear-disarmament/) This is incredibly important that a country which once possessed world-ending bombs gave them up. This shows that other countries can follow suit and dismantle their nuclear programs.
