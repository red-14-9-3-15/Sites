How the Algerian War Ended French Imperialism

Algeria, today the largest country in Africa, shares a colonial past like many other African nations, marked by exploitation and denied sovereignty. Colonized by France in 1830, Algeria endured 132 years of oppressive foreign rule. During this time, settlers dominated the land, and the French government established military governance. The roots of resistance began as early as the 1870s, and over time, Algerians grew more unified in their opposition. The resulting war for independence would be one of the most violent and consequential decolonization struggles of the 20th century. This case study essay explores the historical background, key developments, and lasting implications of the Algerian War of Independence, highlighting how French colonialism's collapse in Algeria reflected broader Cold War and decolonization tensions.

France’s colonization of Algeria in 1830 marked the start of over a century of foreign dominance. By the 1870s, European settlers increasingly sought land and displaced Algerian villagers, provoking resistance. French forces quickly crushed an early uprising, but it set the tone for persistent unrest. Nearly a third of the Algerian population is estimated to have died between the French invasion and the end of the early fighting in the 1870s. The Europeans dominated all aspects of Algeria, including political, economic, and social control. Although the French established new lines of communication, hospitals, and educational facilities, they were more available to Europeans. They only operated in French, making it hard for Algerians to use those services. Primary education was also only offered to Algerians (source).

The Algerian War began on November 1st, 1954, with a group known as the National Liberation Front or FLN’s Toussaint Rouge attacks or “Red All Saints Day.” The French government dismissed the attacks as terrorism, but the FLN’s declarations of “restoring the Algerian state in its sovereign, democratic, and social form” (source)​ proved otherwise. French authorities reacted with what historian Alistair Horne described as “a near-hysterical commitment to maintaining Algeria at any cost” (source), and Prime Minister Pierre Mendés France publicly declared, “Algeria is France,” both expressing the denial that would extend the war. The internal attachments of colonialism and nationality in postwar France have been highlighted by its inability to take Algerian autonomy into account, which went beyond simple political reluctance.

The FLN successfully evoked a disproportionate reaction as part of their strategy. The French military killed thousands of civilians in retaliation for the 1955 Philippeville assaults. This massacre radicalized many Algerians. “Give us your planes and tanks, and we’ll give you our baskets,” FLN leader Larbi Ben M’Hidi subsequently stated. His remarks expressed the moral contention that colonial power could never be just and a somber acceptance of the FLN's asymmetrical situation.

By 1956, the war had shifted to the cities. The Battle of Algiers, which lasted from 1956 to 1957, was one of the most pivotal events in the war. During the battle, the FLN bombed French civilian targets, leading the French army to start mass-arresting people, impose curfews, and torture Algerians. These methods, especially torture, proved to be a moral and political disaster. General Jacques Massu later admitted, “If I had to start over again, I would refuse. Torture is not worth a single drop of French blood.” This admission highlighted the problem France had during their war effort—trying to preserve their country, France undermined its values as a nation. The effects of the torture included major international condemnation, especially after journalists exposed the use of waterboarding and electric shocks that the French used on the FLN.

Meanwhile, the FLN expanded its diplomatic campaign, framing the Algerian cause as part of a global decolonization movement. They were seen on the world stage as the rightful representatives of a repressed people​. Even within France, support began to waver. By 1958, the war had severely destabilized the nation of France. Under pressure from the Algerian crisis, the Fourth Republic collapsed, leading to the return of Charles de Gaulle to power. Many thought that de Gaulle would preserve “French Algeria,” but over time, his intentions of self-determination became clear. This sparked outrage among European settlers in Algeria and military officers, causing the Week of Barricades in January of 1960. During the Week of Barricades, French colonists took to the streets of Algiers in a mini insurrection, shouting “Long live French Algeria!” but the army refused to support the French settlers completely. This exemplifies the breakdown of colonial unity, effectively making the project of maintaining Algeria as a French state no longer a sustainable option. The nail in the coffin came in April 1961, when four high-ranking officers tried to overthrow the French government to stop decolonization, known as the Generals’ Putsch. De Gaulle appeared later on national television, urging loyalty and saying, “A military power cannot be allowed to override the will of the Republic.” His calm authority ended the coup but also confirmed that Algeria was lost. The putsch revealed the depth of the fracture in French society because of the Algerian War.

Negotiations between France and Algeria were signed on March 18, 1962; the Évian Accords marked the war's official end. The treaty stated that Algeria would become independent but maintain economic and cultural ties with France. When the referendum took place on July 1st, 1962, over 99% of Algerians voted for independence. But peace did not come easily. Violence between the FLN factions and pro-French Algerians resulted in tens of thousands of deaths among Algerians. Despite many promises made by France, it only evacuated a fraction of the people. Meanwhile, around 900,000 European settlers fled to France and were suspected because of the horrible things many settlers did to gain more land in Algeria.

 Even though the war ended, Algeria stayed heavily militarized and politically repressive, especially when the FLN started transforming the country into a one-party state. The war was not officially recognized as over until 1999, when the French National Assembly finally acknowledged the conflict as a “war” rather than an “operation to maintain order” (source)​.
