Case Study Essay - The Space Race

How great scientific revolutions came from the stars during a silent war.

Sputnik was the start of a new era: the Space Race. Yuri Gagarin was the first human being to leave Earth[1]. It was because of the Luna missions that we’ve seen the dark side of the moon.[2] Space was being discovered at a faster rate than ever before; however, it was the soviets advancing it all. The US was behind: their satellite came second to Sputnik, Alan Shepard came second to  Yuri Gagarin, and it looked like the Soviets would win. One man, however, changed the outcome in his very famous speech, We Choose to Go to the Moon.[3] That person is John F. Kennedy—at the crux of the Space Race is the one achievement that proved once and for all that capitalism comes out on top: in 1972, the US lands a person on the moon, plants a flag, and leaves footprints that will be remembered forever. It’s all because of Kennedy’s speech that we came together in the span of a decade to do what was impossible for centuries, millennia. Ultimately, the US won the Space Race because of those 25 words: “We choose to go to the Moon in this decade and do the other things, not because they are easy, but because they are hard.” - JFK.

        After World War II, the US and the USSR were in an arms race, started due to the US creation and use of the first and second atomic bombs.[4] Once both countries had proven capable of creating these weapons of mass destruction,  neither could safely launch an attack without initiating the protocols created by both countries for mutually assured destruction.[5] Instead, they went to war silently. Both countries declared that they would send the first artificial satellite into space[6], but the Soviets launched Sputnik 1 on October 4, 1957[7] while the US launched its first on January 31, 1958—Explorer 1.[8]

        Yuri Gagarin was the first person to ever leave Earth in all of human history. Neil Armstrong was the first person ever to set foot on an extraterrestrial body. The Space Race was a time with so many people all doing so many incredible things. From the many NASA engineers to the Soviet cosmonauts, everyone was doing something amazing. John Glenn was the first American to orbit the Earth, and the third American to enter space.[9] Wernher von Braun was “a pioneer of rocket and space technology in the United States” and helped develop the V-2 rocket for the Nazis.[10] 

        With all of these contributions, the world developed like never before. The first intercontinental ballistic missile was created by Von Braun. The USSR launched Sputnik the same year. Spacecrafts are being launched all over the place. Photos of the moon, the Earth, and space are becoming famous. A new revolutionary way to spy on people, spy satellites are changing the game.[11] The list goes on, and all of these achievements have changed how we live today. The Cold War was heavily influenced by it, too, however. For instance, the Cuban Missile Crisis was all because of satellite images that showed missiles miles away from US land. These images wouldn’t have been possible without the Space Race.

        During the Cold War, everyone was starting to feel the effects of nuclear weapons, in both countries. The arms race and threat of nuclear annihilation had become a part of life by the 1950s.[12] When the Soviet Union launched Sputnik from an intercontinental ballistic missile, the US was officially behind and the Space Race had officially started. This was a bad start, and in 1958 the US created NASA to hopefully catch up.[13] Because of this, the conflict got intense as the two sides both tried to top each other peacefully.

Once both sides had declared intents to put a satellite in orbit—and both declaring to before the other, no less—the competition grew. The US and USSR both sought to bring public opinions towards either democracy or socialism, respectively. The feats in rocketry and spaceflight were mainly to attract attention to how their side could accomplish better things sooner while the whole world was watching. In addition to these milestones, technologies used for spaceflight had further applications; rockets could launch missiles, while satellites could keep an eye on enemy territory. [14]

Because this new technology was being developed during the Space Race while the rest of the Cold War was still in progress, satellites were an obvious choice, as they were the first step towards space, and aside from being a political indicator of the more advanced country, satellites could give a military advantage to the country. Satellites are how the US found Soviet missiles on Cuba, and might have been the reason that the US isn’t currently blown up by those same missiles.

The Cold War permanently altered the trajectory of human civilization. The development of nuclear weapons introduced an ever-present tension that defined the global political climate and shaped the Space Race. Kennedy's "We choose to go to the Moon" speech reflected the immense progress of humanity and symbolized the ideological battle between the United States and the Soviet Union. From the launch of Sputnik to the Apollo 11 moon landing, every milestone in space exploration was more than scientific advancement—it was a statement of national identity and ideological superiority.

The Space Race was not a traditional conflict, but a piece of a silent war. Where World War II saw destruction through violence, the space race relied on technological achievement and innovation. Each spaceflight, launch, and discovery was strategically significant, as both nations raced not only for scientific dominance but also for global influence. Satellites became tools of surveillance, and rockets symbols of power. The world was watching, and every victory or failure became a reflection of each side’s capabilities and promises.

Today, the consequences of this era continue to shape geopolitics and technology. The same satellites that once detected missiles in Cuba now drive global communication. The fear of nuclear annihilation has not disappeared, but the cooperation in modern space exploration suggests that the pursuit of knowledge can replace conflict with collaboration. The Space Race demonstrated that humanity’s future may lie in science, not warfare—a lesson that remains just as relevant now as it was then.
